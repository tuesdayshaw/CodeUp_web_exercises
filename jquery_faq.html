National Parks F.A.Q.

1. Who is the director of the National Park Service?
A: Michael T. Reynolds is the acting director of the National Park Service.
2. What government agency oversees the National Park Service?
A: The National Park Service is a bureau of the Department of the Interior. Directly overseeing its operation is the Department's Assistant Secretary for Fish, Wildlife and Parks.
3. How many employees are in the National Park Service?
A: Permanent, temporary, and seasonal: Approximately 22,000 diverse professionals
4. How old is the National Park System?
A: The National Park Service was created by an act signed by President Woodrow Wilson on August 25, 1916. Yellowstone National Park was established by an act signed by President Ulysses S. Grant on March 1, 1872, as the nation's first national park.
5. What is the origin of the National Park Service arrowhead?
A: The arrowhead was authorized as the official National Park Service emblem by the Secretary of the Interior on July 20, 1951. The components of the arrowhead may have been inspired by key attributes of the National Park System, with the sequoia tree and bison representing vegetation and wildlife, the mountains and water representing scenic and recreational values, and the arrowhead itself representing historical and archeological values.
6. How many areas are in the National Park System?
A: The system includes 417 areas covering more than 84 million acres in every state, the District of Columbia, American Samoa, Guam, Puerto Rico, and the Virgin Islands.
7. What is the largest national park site? Smallest?
A: Largest: Wrangell-St. Elias National Park and Preserve, AK, at 13.2 million acres and Smallest: Thaddeus Kosciuszko National Memorial, PA, at 0.02 acres
8. How many people visit the national parks?
A: Total recreation visitors to the national parks in 2016: 330,971,689
9. How do I obtain a park/entrance pass or a park pass for all national parks?
A: Some parks charge an entrance fee. Visit the park's website or contact the park to learn more.
10. Where can I find a park map?
A: You can view maps on specific park websites or download maps from the Harpers Ferry Center map collection.

Grand Canyon

  • The Grand Canyon is found in the US state of Arizona.
  • Many people consider it to be one of the seven wonders of the natural world.
  • The Grand Canyon is 277 miles (446 kilometres) in length.
  • At its widest point the Grand Canyon stretches 18 miles (29 kilometres) across.

Denali

  • Denali National Park is the site of the highest peak on the continent of North America.
  • Denali means "The High One" in language of the Athabascan tribe.
  • Denali National Park is home to an abundance of wildlife, but the most striking inhabitant is probably the wood frog-- the only amphibian to call the park home.
  • Much contemporary knowledge about the behavior of wolves came from studying the animals in Denali National Park.

Everglades

  • Largest continuous stand of sawgrass prairie in North America.
  • Home of thirteen endangered and ten threatened species.
  • Most significant breeding grounds for tropical wading birds in North America.
  • Major "edge" area of the northern and southern limits for many species creating a unique mingling of diverse temperate and subtropical species.
